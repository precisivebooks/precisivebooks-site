# Mobile Notary vs. Courthouse Notary: What's the Difference?

Both a courthouse notary and a mobile notary can apply the same official Virginia notary seal. The notarization itself is legally identical. What's different is **when**, **where**, and **how** you get it done—and which is right for your situation.

Here's a plain comparison.

## Courthouse and bank notaries

The Fairfax County courthouse, Loudoun County courthouse, and most major banks have notaries on staff. These are usually the cheapest option, sometimes free if you're already a customer.

**Pros:**

- Low cost or free
- Walk-in availability at some locations
- Familiar setting

**Cons:**

- **Weekday hours only**, typically 9:00 AM–4:00 PM
- You drive to them, find parking, and wait
- Bank notaries may decline documents that aren't bank-related
- Limited availability (one notary on duty, possibly on lunch)
- Witnesses must travel with you

## Mobile notaries

A mobile notary is a Virginia-commissioned notary who travels to your location—home, office, hospital, care facility, real estate closing table—on a schedule that works for you, including weekends.

**Pros:**

- **Weekends, evenings, and same-day appointments** available
- No driving, parking, or waiting in line
- Witnesses can stay where they are
- Handles complex personal documents the courthouse may refuse
- You get the notary's full attention for the appointment

**Cons:**

- Higher cost—you're paying for travel and convenience
- Requires scheduling (most mobile notaries don't do walk-ins)

## The cost difference, plainly

A courthouse notary in Virginia is capped at a few dollars per stamp. A mobile notary costs more — you're paying for weekend availability, at your location, with no time off work and no driving. If weekday availability works for you and your documents are simple, the courthouse is the budget-friendly choice. If you need to do this on a Saturday morning before your kids' soccer game, a mobile notary is what you want.

## When to choose a mobile notary

A mobile notary makes sense when:

- It's a **weekend** and the courthouse and banks are closed
- The signer is **homebound, elderly, or in a care facility**
- You're handling a **real estate closing** that lands on a weekend
- You have **multiple signers** who can't easily meet at one location during business hours
- You can't take **time off work** during the week
- Your bank's notary **declined** the document
- You have **multiple documents** to notarize at once and want them done in a single visit

## When to choose a courthouse or bank notary

Stick with the courthouse or bank when:

- Your document is simple (a single affidavit, for example)
- You can easily go during the week
- You only need one stamp
- The fee matters more than the convenience

## Bottom line

Same legal notarization. Different delivery. Choose based on your schedule and how much your time is worth.
